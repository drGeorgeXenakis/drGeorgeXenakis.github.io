Abstract

Sitka spruce is the major conifer species in British upland forests and is predominantly managed as even-aged, single-species plantations with rotation lengths of less than 50 years using a “patch clear-felling" system. Evidence on the impact of clear-felling on the carbon, water, and energy balances of plantation forestry is sparse and extreme weather events, such as the 2018 drought that occurred across Europe, are expected to have considerable impact. We used energy balance and gas flux data from a typical mature stand and a clear-felled site on peaty-gley soil at Harwood Forest in Northumberland, UK, over 4 years to quantify the impact of clear-felling and investigate how the drought of 2018 affected these fluxes. Felling resulted in an annual \(\rm CO_{2}\) source (7.05 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)) of similar magnitude in absolute terms to the mature stand sink (-7.09 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)). The source was reduced with the growth of ground vegetation, and after tree replanting two years post-felling, the site was a weak source. Felling increased reflected radiation by 92%, reduced evapotranspiration by 28% and therefore increased site wetness by 8%, while replanting and the spread of ground vegetation increased evapotranspiration by 16% on average. The warm, dry spring and summer in 2018 increased evapotranspiration and reduced net carbon uptake of the mature stand by 30% compared to the mean of the previous three years, because of a small decrease in photosynthetic uptake and a small increase in ecosystem respiration. The drought conditions also increased ecosystem losses of \(\rm CO_{2}\) at the clear-felled site by 28%. The clear-fell site would have turned into a small \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink in the second year after replanting, if there had not been a drought.

Keywords: Sitka spruce, Net Ecosystem Exchange, Forest management, Clear-felling, Drought, Albedo, Water use efficiency, Bowen ratio

Introduction

The climate is changing and increasing mean temperatures and carbon dioxide (\(\rm CO_{2}\)) concentrations are affecting the carbon cycle of forests at global (Beer et al. 2010) and regional scales (Reichstein et al. 2013) by substantially altering their exchange of \(\rm CO_{2}\) with the atmosphere (Forkel et al. 2016). Globally, forests play an important role in mitigating climate change (Luyssaert et al. 2018), through removing 30% per year of the anthropogenic \(\rm CO_{2}\) emissions from the atmosphere (Le Quéré et al. 2015). However, forest removal contributes to an estimated 9% of the global \(\rm CO_{2}\) emissions (Le Quéré et al. 2015). Furthermore, there is also the debate as to what is their impact on global radiative forcing with some suggesting that in the boreal zone, the substantial decrease in albedo with increased tree cover offsets the mitigation benefits from carbon uptake (Betts 2000; Bonan 2008). In the temperate zone Luyssaert et al. (2014) showed that understanding the net impact of land management changes on surface temperature depends on the balance between albedo and sensible heat flux changes.

In the UK forests cover 3.2 \(\rm Mha\) of woodlands and forests, covering 13% of the land surface (Forestry Commission 2019), with the UK LULUCF inventory estimating a net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake by forests of 17 \(\rm MtCO_2\) in 2014 (BEIS 2016). Approximately half of the UK forest area is coniferous forests with about 50% of that area fast growing Sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis (Bong.) Carr.) (Forestry Commission 2019). About 6,809 \(\rm km^{2}\) of spruce forests are on high organic carbon peaty gley and peaty podzol soils with a mean total carbon stocks of 362 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\), 75% of which is found within the first 40 \(\rm cm\) depth (Vanguelova et al. 2013).These forests are commonly managed as even-aged, single-species plantations with a rotation length of commonly 40-50 years depending on wind climate, using a “patch clear-felling” harvesting system (Mason and Perks 2011; Matthews 1989) where patches of 10-30 \(\rm ha\) are clear-felled and then replanted at a density of approximately 2500 trees \(\rm ha^{-1}\)(Low 1987; McIntosh 1995). This high intensity management system is expected to have a large impact on the dynamics of carbon, water, and energy fluxes of these forests.

Several studies have aimed to quantify the impact of patch clear-felling and consequent replanting on carbon stocks and budgets (Ball, Smith, and Moncrieff 2007; Zerva et al. 2005; Zerva and Mencuccini 2005; Paul-Limoges et al. 2015), and temporal variability in carbon sink-source dynamics (Hargreaves, Milne, and Cannell 2003; Jarvis and Linder 2007; Jarvis et al. 2009) and other greenhouse gases emissions (Mojeremane, Rees, and Mencuccini 2010, 2012). While the impact of multiple rotations on stand carbon balance was assessed through modelling (Minunno et al. 2010), the model poorly represents the impact of clear-felling. Thus, there is still a considerable knowledge gap regarding the short- and long-term impact of the harvest-replant stage on carbon, energy and water fluxes in the forest management cycle (Jarvis and Linder 2007).

Many studies have also previously reported the energy fluxes and Bowen ratio (Wilson et al. 2003; Williams et al. 2013; Amiro et al. 2006; Law et al. 2002; Grünwald and Bernhofer 2007) or measurements of albedo (Betts and Ball 1997; Lutz and Howarth 2014) of several types of forest ecosystems, but only a few have reported the impact of clear-felling on energy fluxes and albedo (Amiro et al. 2006; Korkiakoski et al. 2019; Williams et al. 2013). In Britain, only the study by Clement (2004) has previously examined the complete carbon, water, and energy balance of Sitka spruce forest on organo-mineral soil. However, there is still a lack of evidence on the impact of clear-felling on all three aspects of intensively managed forest plantations.

In recent years we have also seen large variability in global and local weather patterns. In particular, drought events are having a negative effect on terrestrial ecosystems (Schwalm et al. 2012; Bastos et al. 2020). In Europe in 2018 for example, a warm spring followed by an extremely dry and hot summer caused water depletion and reduction in ecosystem carbon fluxes (Bastos et al. 2020). Under future climate change scenarios drought is predicted to increase both in duration and frequency (Touma et al. 2015; Cook, Ault, and Smerdon 2015) and recovery to a pre-drought condition is highly dependent on climate and carbon cycle dynamics (Schwalm et al. 2017). Currently we are lacking evidence on the effect of drought at different stages in the management cycle in Sitka spruce forests.

The aims of this research were to quantify the impact of the clear-felling and replanting management system for upland Sitka spruce forest in Britain by comparison of carbon, water, and energy balances of a clear-fell site with a closed-canopy stand over 4 years. During the study a substantial drought occurred, which provided additional information about the sensitivity of these balances and underlying processes to drought. In this paper we ask: 1) How did clear-felling influence the \(\rm CO_{2}\) source/sink dynamics in comparison to the closed-canopy stand? 2) How was the site energy and water balance affected by felling? 3) How many years are needed for the clear-fell area to turn from \(\rm CO_{2}\) source to sink? 4) How did drought affect the sink/source dynamics for carbon, water, and energy at the two sites?

Material and methods

Flux sites

Two eddy covariance (EC) flux and microclimate monitoring sites were established in an extensive commercial upland conifer forest block in Harwood Forest, Northumberland, NE England: one measuring above the canopy of a mature stand and the second on a recently clear-felled stand only 2 \(\rm km\) distant. Prior to clear-felling, both stands were of similar age, productivity, climatic and edaphic conditions, enabling the effect of clear-fell management to be inferred from their comparison. The soil type was peaty-gley (cambic stagnohumic gley, WRB, FAO). Mean annual temperature for a nearby location (8 \(\rm km\) from the site) between 1981-2011 was 7.6 \(\rm ^{o}C\) and mean annual precipitation was 904 \(\rm mm\)(Met Office, UK).

Mature forest site

The mature forest site (hereafter referred to as \(\rm MS\)) is in an extensive 40 \(\rm ha\) area of plantation forest. The stand was planted in 1973 and is a second rotation, even-aged, mature Sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis (Bong.) Carr.) stand, with a yield class of 18 \(\mathrm{m^3\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}}\) with a top height of 26 \(\rm m\), leaf area index of 5.7 and mean tree density of 1348 trees \(\rm ha^{-1}\). The site has an elevation of 290 m and a slope of 2\(^{o}\).

The tower was installed in the summer of 2013 and extended in 2014 to a top height of 32 \(\rm m\) above ground. A modified, semi-enclosed IRGA (LI7500, Li-Cor Inc., Lincoln NE, USA; Clement et al. 2009) with a 1.5 \(\rm m\) insulated (unheated) metal intake tube and a high airflow rate fan (60 \(\rm L\;min^{-1}\), D484T, Micronel, UK), and a 3D sonic anemometer (CSAT-3, Campbell Scientific Ltd, Shepshed, Leicestershire, UK), were installed at 34 \(\rm m\) height on the top of the tower, both collecting at 10 \(\rm Hz\) frequency. The sonic anemometer was orientated towards the prevailing wind direction, with the IRGA intake tube placed vertically adjacent to the anemometer and shielded to prevent rain and detritus entering the enclosure. Meteorological variables including mean air temperature and humidity (HMP60, Campbell Scientific Ltd.), precipitation (RG2 rain gauge, Delta-T Devices Ltd, Burwell, Cambridge, UK), wind speed with cup anemometers (A100L2, Vector Instruments, Rhyl, UK), incoming and outgoing short and long wave radiation (CNR4, Kipp & Zonen, Netherlands), photosynthetic active radiation (SKP215, Skye Instruments Ltd, Llandrindod Wells, Powys, UK) and surface wetness (SKLW 1900, Skye Instruments Ltd), were recorded every 5 seconds from the top of the tower. To calculate the \(\rm CO_{2}\) storage term below the EC instruments, air was sampled in a vertical profile of eight points at 0.3, 1, 4, 8, 14, 20, 26 and 30 \(\rm m\) above ground level. Samples were drawn sequentially (from base to top inlet) using a pump with 10 \(\rm L\;min^{-1}\) flow rate connected to a custom-built multiplexer operated by a Raspberry Pi computer (Model B, Raspberry Pi Foundation, UK). Air sampled from each height passed to a closed-path IRGA (LI6262, Li-Cor Inc.) for measuring \(\rm CO_{2}\) and \(\rm H_{2}O\) concentration. Each height was sampled for 1 minute and 40 seconds and concentrations were recorded every second, with a 13 minute measurement cycle. Additional measurements include: soil water content (CS605, Campbell Scientific Ltd) at an average depth of 0.3 \(\rm m\) and soil temperature (107 probe, Campbell Scientific Ltd) at four depths (0.02, 0.1, 0.3, and 0.6 \(\rm m\)); soil heat flux (HFP01, Hukseflux, Netherlands); wind speed (A100L2, Vector Instruments) and wind direction (W200P, Vector Instruments) at the eight measurement points of the \(\rm CO_{2}\) profile; and below-canopy air temperature and humidity at 2.5 \(\rm m\) above ground (HMP60, Campbell Scientific Ltd.). All data were logged using CR1000 and CR3000 data loggers (Campbell Scientific Ltd.). Above-canopy, downward facing hemispherical photographs (Coolpix 4300, Nikon, UK) of the canopy were recorded using a Raspberry Pi computer (Model B, Raspberry Pi Foundation, UK).

In June 2018 a second 10 \(\rm Hz\) EC system was installed at 3 \(\rm m\) height in the bole space under the canopy, 50 \(\rm m\) from the main tower in a West-South-West direction. The system was equipped with an open-path IRGA (LI7500A, Li-Cor Inc.,), a 3D sonic anemometer (CSAT-3, Campbell Scientific Ltd) with an air temperature and humidity sensor (HMP60) and a net radiometer (CNR4). The sonic anemometer was also oriented towards the prevailing wind direction.

Clear-fell site

The clear-fell site (hereafter referred to as \(\rm CFS\)) was established two kilometres North-East of \(\rm MS\). The site has an elevation of 280 \(\rm m\) and a slope of 5\(^{o}\) from the north to the south. Prior to clear-felling, the 42 \(\rm ha\) area was predominately a first rotation mature Sitka spruce planted in between 1955 and 1958 on 25.9 \(\rm ha\)(77% of area), with Scots pine (Pinus sylvestris, L.), Lodgepole pine (Pinus contorta, L.) and Norway spruce (Picea abies, L.) on 3.2, 3.1 and 1.6 \(\rm ha\), respectively, and the remainder (8.2 \(\rm ha\)) open ground areas. Yield class ranged between 12 and 18 \(\mathrm{m^3\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}}\) and the number of stems between 934 and 2000 \(\rm ha^{-1}\). The main stand of interest was planted in 1958 and had a yield class of 16 \(\mathrm{m^3\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}}\) and density of 1375 trees \(\rm ha^{-1}\). Felling started in late January 2015 and was completed by early March 2015. Operations followed the standard practice of the Forestry Commission (Murgatroyd and Saunders 2005) and only large timber (\(>\) 7 \(\rm cm\) diameter) was removed from site. Tree tops, branches and needles were left on site in rows with some used as brash-mats to prevent soil compaction from the harvesting machinery. In February 2017 replanting with 2-year old seedlings across the footprint of the tower began after ground preparation (by excavator mounding and ancillary drainage ditch maintenance). Initially after felling the ground surface was only covered with the litter layers from the previous stand and woody brash rows, but over the 4 years of measurements ground vegetation of grasses, sedges, rushes, and additional naturally regenerating spruce seedlings slowly recolonised most areas.

A 6 \(\rm m\) tower (Gigalite 4, Lite Structures Ltd., Pontefract, UK) was installed in June 2015. The tower was instrumented with a second semi-enclosed modified IRGA and a CSAT-3 3D sonic anemometer collecting data at 10 \(\rm Hz\). The installation of the IRGA was similar to that of the \(\rm MS\) tower, but with the IRGA tube intake on the west side of the anemometer centre aligned to the prevailing wind. The same type of instruments and data loggers were used as detailed for the \(\rm MS\).

In addition, an independent automatic weather station was set up 100 \(\rm m\) north of the tower recording all basic meteorological information including mean air temperature and humidity (HC2S3, Campbell Scientific Ltd.) at 2 \(\rm m\), precipitation (ARG100, Campbell Scientific Ltd), incoming and outgoing total solar radiation (CMP3, Kipp & Zonen), wind speed and wind direction from a two-dimensional sonic anemometer (Windsonic, Gill Instruments, Lymington, Hampshire, UK) and soil temperature at 0.02, 0.3 and 0.6 \(\rm m\) depth (107 probe, Campbell Scientific, USA).

There are three adjacent tree stands within 500 \(\rm m\) of the \(\rm CFS\), two with young Sitka spruce (11 years old, approximately 3.5 \(\rm m\) tall at the time) on the North-West and North side and a Scots pine stand approximately 18 \(\rm m\) tall to the South-East of the tower. However, the majority of the clear-felled area is to the South-West of the tower and in the predominant wind direction.

Data processing and flux calculations

Turbulent flux

Half-hourly fluxes of sensible and latent heat, momentum, carbon dioxide and water vapour were calculated from the high frequency data using EddyPro v5.2 (LiCor Inc.). Meteorological data were also averaged for each half hour and used for corrections of the calculated fluxes. Sonic anemometer velocity coordinates were corrected for tilt using the planar fit using the method of Wilczak, Oncley, and Stage (2001) for \(\rm MS\) and double rotation for \(\rm CFS\). To calculate the rotation matrices, two sectors were used with a -15\(^{o}\) offset from the North; one of 140\(^{o}\) towards the prevailing wind and one of 220\(^{o}\). The first sector therefore included mainly data from the side of the tower where the structure did not obstruct the airflow while the second sector contained the remaining. Two years of data were used for the calculations. Turbulent fluctuations were corrected using a block average de-trending algorithm. Fluxes were corrected for density fluctuations using WPL correction (Webb, Pearman, and Leuning 1980). Spectral corrections were also applied to calculated half-hourly fluxes for low and high frequencies using the analytical methods of Moncrieff et al. (1997) and Moncrieff et al. (2004), respectively. Time-lag compensation correction was applied using the “covariance maximisation with default” option. The default time-lag was calculated based on the length of the sampling tube (1.5 \(\rm m\)) and the airflow rate of the fan. After processing and corrections, the quality of the fluxes was flagged using the Mauder and Foken (2004) three class system. Air temperature, net radiation, and photosynthetic photon flux density were gap-filled before processing of fluxes, using data from the independent automatic weather station.

\(\rm CO_{2}\) storage flux

Net ecosystem exchange at the \(\rm CFS\) consisted only of turbulent flux as the storage below the EC instruments was assumed negligible. At the mature stand the storage flux was calculated from \(\rm CO_{2}\) concentrations measured sequentially at different heights below and through the canopy at 1 second frequency. Values were averaged for every 1 minute and 40 seconds for each measurement height. Because of the sequential nature of measurements and the 13 minute cycle between the bottom and the top measurement, mixing ratio was not available for all heights at the beginning and end of each 30 minute period. To address this issue, we implemented the methodology described by Yang et al. (2007) to derive a pure interpolation of data between available measurements (Equation[eq:storage]).

\[F_{s} = \overline{\rho(h)} \times \int_{0}^{h} \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}dz = \overline{\rho(h)} \times \sum_{i=1}^{8} \frac{\Delta Ci}{\Delta t}h_{i} \label{eq:storage}\]

For each 30 minute period the storage flux (\(F_{s}\), \(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) was calculated as the difference in \(\rm CO_{2}\) mixing ratio multiplied by the depth of each layer (\(h\), \(\rm m\)), where \(z\) is the height (\(\rm m\)) of the measurement point, which was the centre of the layer, \(\overline{\rho(h)}\) is the mean air density (\(\rm kPa\)) across measurement layer and \(t\) is time. Air density was calculated using the ideal gas law.

Canopy decoupling and flux correction

Preliminary data analysis from the \(\rm MS\) suggested an overestimation of net \(\rm CO_{2}\) fluxes compared with available published literature. Thomas et al. (2013) previously had found a strong canopy decoupling effect in a high leaf area index Douglas fir stand causing an overestimation of net ecosystem exchange by instruments above the canopy. Decoupling of fluxes at different heights occurs when the fluxes measured above the canopy do not include \(\rm CO_{2}\) fluxes below the canopy due to lateral air movement and different airflow below and above the canopy caused by the high leaf area and large \(\rm CO_{2}\) gradient (Jocher et al. 2017, 2018; Thomas et al. 2013). This is likely to overestimate net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake as respiratory effluxes from soil and stems will not be correctly captured. To investigate if canopy decoupling was occurring in the mature stand, we used data from the sub-canopy EC system collected between June 2018 and February 2019 and examined wind directions above and below the canopy. Evidence of decoupling was found (see results section) and hence measured net fluxes needed correction when this occurred. We followed the methodology described by Thomas et al. (2013) and further expanded by Jocher et al. (2017, 2018). To identify these periods, we examined the relationship between the log of the standard deviation of the vertical wind velocity from the below- (\(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\), \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\)) and above- (\(\sigma_{w_{top}}\), \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\)) canopy sonic anemometers. When the canopy is fully coupled the relationship is linear, with a break-point when flow changes from fully coupled to decoupled (Thomas et al. 2013). Half-hourly values of \(\sigma_{w_{top}}\) were binned and the mean value of \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) was calculated for each bin. To account for stable conditions during night-time, we split the data into day and night based on a 20 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\) threshold of global incoming shortwave radiation (Ruppert et al. 2006). To identify the break point of the linear relationship between \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) and \(\sigma_{w_{top}}\) in a statistical rather than a visual manner, two linear regressions were fitted to the binned data; one using point \([1,k]\) and second from point \([k,n_{tot}]\) where \(n_{tot}\) is the total number of bins and \(k\in{1,n_{tot}}\). The process was repeated for \(n_{tot}-1\) times with \(k\) increasing in steps of one and for each iteration the root mean square error of each regression was calculated. The threshold (\(\sigma_{w_{th}}\)) was selected as the point at which the sum of the root mean square errors of the two regressions was at its minimum. The process was repeated separately for day and night data. Net ecosystem exchange was then calculated using Equation[eq:nee]:

\[NEE = \begin{cases} F_{CO_{2}}+F_{s}+R_{e_{sub}} & \texttt{\rm if}\ \sigma_{w_{top}} \leq \sigma_{w_{th}} \\ F_{CO_{2}}+F_{s} & \texttt{\rm if}\ \sigma_{w_{top}} > \sigma_{w_{th}} \end{cases} \label{eq:nee}\]

where \(NEE\)(\(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) is the net ecosystem exchange, \(F_{CO2}\)(\(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) is the turbulent flux, \(F_{s}\)(\(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) is the storage flux and \(R_{e_{sub}}\)(\(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) is the ecosystem respiration of the sub-canopy. Because of the high stem density and leaf area index of the stand, there was no under-story vegetation and so gross primary production of the sub-canopy was assumed negligible.

To calculate \(R_{e_{sub}}\) we used the modified relaxed eddy accumulation (REA) method proposed by Thomas et al. (2008) which is based on the traditional representation of REA but using high frequency measurements. In addition, measurements of water vapour density perturbations provide an additional identifier for up-drafts that are enriched both in \(\rm CO_{2}\) and \(\rm H_{2}O\) from other up-draft events. Raw EC data in each half-hour period were split into 6 minute intervals and ecosystem respiration was calculated using the formulae given by Thomas et al. (2008). Half-hour \(R_{e_{sub}}\) was calculated as the sum of all 6 minute intervals. Spikes due to either instrument noise or rain blocking the IRGA mirror were removed manually.

\[R_{e_{sub}}=R_{10} \times e^{E_0\times\Big(\dfrac{1}{283.15-T_0}-\dfrac{1}{T-T_0}\bigg)} \label{eq:Arrhenius}\]

To gap-fill years with no sub-canopy EC data available, we used an Arrhenius type model to estimate \(R_{e_{sub}}\) using below-canopy air temperature as originally proposed for modelling soil respiration by Lloyd and Taylor (1994). Data were binned into 124 temperature bins based on the range of values, and mean \(R_{e_{sub}}\) and air temperature below the canopy was calculated for each bin. The model of Equation[eq:Arrhenius] was fitted to the binned data, where \(R_{10}\) (\(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) is the respiration rate at 10 \(\rm ^{o}C\), \(E_{0}\) is the activation energy (308.56 \(\rm K\)), \(T_{0}\) is a reference temperature (227.13 \(\rm K\)) and \(T\) (\(\rm K\)) is the air temperature.

Quality checks, filtering and gap-filling

Half-hourly turbulent fluxes were manually cleaned for periods when there was a sensor malfunction or values were considered biologically implausible. Then, storage flux and below-canopy ecosystem respiration were added to the turbulent flux. Subsequently, we removed all half-hourly carbon and energy fluxes flagged as QC=2 by the Mauder and Foken (2004) method. We then removed spikes of both carbon and energy fluxes using a two-step process, part of a custom made R package (FREddyPro; Xenakis 2016). Because of the statistical nature of the de-spiking method, to account for seasonal difference in the magnitude of fluxes, data were split into three 4-month periods (January to April, May to August and September to December) with each period including data from all four years. In the first stage, data were split into night and day using a threshold of 20 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\) of global shortwave radiation (Reichstein et al. 2005). Then, the mean flux and standard deviation for day and night was calculated for each 4 month period and any half-hour exceeding three times the standard deviation was considered a spike and rejected. In the second stage, data from each of the 48 half-hours were grouped together and the 5th and 95th percentile was calculated with any half-hour outside these percentiles rejected. Data were further filtered to remove stable wind conditions using a friction velocity threshold (\(u^{*}\), \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\)) calculated using the methodology of Papale et al. (2006) implemented in the R package “REddyProc”. Seasonal \(u^{*}\) thresholds were calculated for each year to account for any trends.

After filtering, data were gap-filled using the method of Reichstein et al. (2005) and partitioned using the method of Lasslop et al. (2010), applied using the R package “REddyProc”. In early 2017 a large gap (26 days) in the data due to instrument malfunction was not filled by the Reichstein et al. (2005) method and so a regression approached was used. To gap-fill the carbon fluxes we first split the complete dataset into day-time and night-time with a global radiation threshold of 20 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)(Ruppert et al. 2006). We then further split the day-time data into 5 temperature classes and within each temperature class we binned the data into 60 radiation classes for which the median \(NEE\) was calculated. A Michaelis-Menten model was then fitted (Equation[eq:lightCurve]) to the binned data against global radiation (\(R_{g}\), \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)), with \(\alpha\) being a parameter, \(Q_{sat}\) the saturation point of the curve and \(R\) the respiration component.

\[NEE=\frac{a \times Q_{sat} \times R_{g}}{Q_{sat}+a \times R_{g}}+R \label{eq:lightCurve}\]

For night-time periods, we binned the data into 14 classes and calculated the median before fitting the Arrhenius respiration model of Equation[eq:Arrhenius]. Sensible heat flux was gap-filled using the same approach as the day-time net ecosystem exchange, fitting a linear model with radiation for each temperature class. Latent heat was gap-filled similarly by fitting a linear model with binned radiation but without a further split into temperature classes. Over the 4 years, 46% of \(\rm CO_{2}\), 52% of latent heat and 35% of sensible heat half-hour values were gap-filled.

All quality checks, filtering and gap-filling procedures were similar for both the \(\rm MS\) and \(\rm CFS\). However, because of the location of the \(\rm CFS\) tower and to avoid contamination from any surrounding stands, a further filtering of the data for wind direction was necessary. From UAV aerial photography taken in June 2015, we geo-referenced a polar grid split in sectors of 22.5\(^{o}\) from which we selected three between 210 – 270\(^{o}\) that faced the extended clear-felled area without any trees. Data were gap-filled after filtering for wind direction. To complete the annual \(NEE\) budget for 2015 we used the first month of measurements, which we assume was only ecosystem respiration, to build a regression model with air temperature and gap fill for missing months. Using temperature data from \(\rm MS\), fluxes were binned, and Equation[eq:Arrhenius] fitted. Ecosystem respiration was then calculated from the beginning of 2015 until the start of actual measurements (June 2015). To complete the timeseries of all key meteorological variables for 2015 at the \(\rm CFS\), except for evapotranspiration, we used data from the \(\rm MS\). To gap-fill evapotranspiration, we developed a linear relationship with binned global radiation from available 2015 measurements.

Soil measurements and partitioning ecosystem respiration

To partition ecosystem respiration into above-ground and soil, we conducted a series of manual surveys of total soil respiration (heterotrophic and autotrophic, \(R_{s}\), \(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)), using a portable IRGA with 20 cm diameter chamber (LI8100A and LI8100-103, LiCor Inc.) and permanent soil collars at both sites and calculated the soil:ecosystem respiration ratio (\(r_{R}\)). Surveys took place every two weeks between 2015 and 2017 and every month in 2018, but between the end of July 2015 and end of August 2015 \(R_{s}\) was measured twice a week. At each site before the first measurements we randomly located 25 plastic collars each one with a diameter of 20 cm and an offset of \(\rm \sim 5 cm\) (volume \(\rm \sim 4843 cm^3\)). Each collar was measured for 1 minute and 20 seconds with 20 seconds pre- and post-purge. The full set of measurements lasted 2 1/2 hours per site, conducted on the same day either side of midday and the order of sampling between sites was rotated between occasions. Fluxes for each collar were calculated fitting a linear regression between \(\rm CO_{2}\) mixing ratio and time. The mean \(r_{R}\) was calculated for each year by averaging the ratio of the mean soil respiration of each survey and mean ecosystem respiration during the same time. For consistency with the filtering of eddy covariance data, the ratio for the \(\rm CFS\) was calculated using collars within the 210 – 270\(^{o}\) wind direction. Although we calculated the ratio for each year at the \(\rm CFS\), ground vegetation colonised the collars over time, but to avoid adding bias due to soil disturbance and added root decomposition, new vegetation was not removed. For partitioning fluxes at the \(\rm CFS\), we used the 2015 \(r_{R}\) value across all four years, with gap-filled ecosystem respiration from the start of 2015 to the beginning of actual measurements assumed to represent soil respiration. At the \(\rm MS\) we used the ratio for each year.

Further calculations

We assessed the energy balance closure (\(EBC\)) as the slope of the linear relationship between the sum of daily sensible heat (\(H\), \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)) and latent heat (\(LE\), \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)) versus the difference between daily net radiation (\(R_{n}\), \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)) and soil heat flux (\(G\), \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\)) using data before gap filling, but after quality control and \(u^{*}\) threshold filtering. We also calculated the monthly sum of day-time energy fluxes, mean monthly Bowen ratio (\(\beta\), unitless) as the ratio of \(H\) to \(LE\), and mean monthly albedo as the ratio of outgoing to incoming shortwave radiation (\(\alpha\), unitless).

We calculated the mean monthly water use efficiency at site level by analogy to the leaf-scale ratio of photosynthesis to transpiration as the ratio of gross primary photosynthesis (\(GPP\), \(\rm\mu mol\,CO_{2}\;\allowbreak m^{-2}\;s^{-1}\)) to total losses of water through evapotranspiration (\(ET\)). However, at large scales the ratio becomes de-coupled from its immediate dependency with leaf stomatal conductance, which leads to small changes in severe droughts (Reichstein et al. 2007).

\[iW_{E}=\frac{GPP\times D}{ET} \label{eq:iwue}\]

As an alternative, we used the method proposed by Beer et al. (2009) and calculated the “inherent water use efficiency” (Equation[eq:iwue]; \(iW_{E}\), \(\rm gC\,\allowbreak hPa\;kgH_{2}O^{-1}\)) which adjusts the ratio by multiplying with vapour pressure deficit (\(D\), \(\rm kPa\)). To reduce the contribution of soil evaporation and interception to the total \(ET\), so that \(iW_{E}\) reflects more closely the photosynthetic to stomatal conductance ratio, data from rainy days and two subsequent days were excluded from the analysis. This is based on the assumption that soil and canopy evaporation declines rapidly after a rainfall event (Beer et al. 2009).

Results

Site conditions

Climate

Mean monthly air temperatures were very similar between the mature stand (\(\rm MS\)) and clear-fell (\(\rm CFS\)) sites across all four years (Figure1a). There was an increase of 2 \(\rm ^{o}C\) in July and 1 \(\rm ^{o}C\) in August 2018 compared to the same months of the previous three years. The winter of 2017/2018 was considerably colder than the previous years, but the mean annual temperature of 2018 was not different from the long-term mean (7.7 \(\rm ^{o}C\)). Mean monthly surface soil temperatures (\(T_{sf}\), \(\rm ^{o}C\)) were similar between years and between sites for the first three years, with slightly higher values at the clear-fell site in spring and early summer. Between May and August 2018, the mean \(T_{sf}\) at the \(\rm CFS\) was 0.7 \(\rm ^{o}C\) higher than the mean of the previous three years for the same period, but it was unchanged at the \(\rm MS\). Mean monthly precipitation (Figure1c) was always higher at the \(\rm MS\), which resulted from differences in both raingauge location (atop the 32 \(\rm m\) tower above the forest canopy compared to mounted on the ground) and elevation and exposure between the two sites. November 2015 to January 2016 were particularly high precipitation months with snowfalls in November of over 250 \(\rm mm\) equivalent. The wettest year at the \(\rm MS\) was in 2017 (1439 \(\rm mm\)), whereas at the \(\rm CFS\) it was in 2016 (1159 \(\rm mm\)). Although the annual precipitation for 2018 was only 14% (1010 \(\rm mm\)) and 12% (962 \(\rm mm\)) lower than the mean for the 3 previous years at the \(\rm MS\) and \(\rm CFS\) respectively, May, June and July 2018 were drier than the previous years (lowest in May), which when associated with high photon flux densities (Figure1e) because of the reduced cloud cover, produced high evapotranspiration rates leading to very dry soil conditions (Figure1d). Vapour pressure deficit was also consistently higher at the \(\rm CFS\) during the summer months than at the \(\rm MS\) except for a small period of 3 months during spring of 2016 (Figure1e).

Wind direction and footprint

The prevailing wind was from the West and West-South-West directions for both sites. In the prevailing wind direction, wind speed at the top of the 6m tower was frequently slightly higher at the \(\rm CFS\) because of the exposure of the anemometer compared to that above the canopy at the \(\rm MS\)(Figure3a,b). Footprint analysis using the Kormann and Meixner (2001) model (Figure3c) showed the four year average cumulative footprint at the \(\rm MS\) had a more westerly direction than at the \(\rm CFS\), with 70% of the fluxes coming from within 250 m and 500 m respectively. The footprint at the \(\rm CFS\) was mainly within the clear-felled area, but at times fluxes were received from the surrounding tree stands from NW, NE and SE direction. Hence to ensure these fluxes were not included in further analysis we applied a directional filter (see materials and methods).

Energy fluxes

The closure of the measured daily energy balance (\(EBC\)) varied between years and sites (Table[tab:ebc]) although the overall four year \(EBC\) was 84% at the mature stand and 91% at the clear-fell site. During the drier 2018 the \(EBC\) increased at both sites (Table[tab:ebc]), which may indicate an alteration in energy balance components over the footprint. The difference in the intercept represents a difference in the systematic error of the closure between the two sites (between -13 and -21 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\) for the \(\rm MS\) and 0.1 and 14 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\) for the \(\rm CFS\)).

The seasonal cycle of mean monthly day-time net radiation was generally higher during summer at the mature site than the clear-fell (Figure6a). Net radiation peaked during May-July at both the mature and clear-fell site (average peak value of 310 and 443 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\) respectively), except in 2017 at the \(\rm CFS\) after re-planting, which peaked in July at 387 \(\rm W\;m^{-2}\). The difference in day-time \(R_{n}\) between the two sites was higher between May and August 2018 due to a 13% higher \(R_{n}\) at the \(\rm MS\) compared to the mean for the same period of the previous 3 years, while \(R_{n}\) at the \(\rm CFS\) was almost unchanged. Mean monthly albedo (\(\alpha\), unitless) at the \(\rm MS\) was consistently around a value of 0.08. The albedo was higher at the \(\rm CFS\), between 0.14 and 0.16, although there is the possibility of an underestimation as measurements were from the automatic weather station 100 \(\rm m\) north of the main tower, on an upslope location with slightly drier soil and less vegetation than the main footprint area. Between May and August 2018, \(\alpha\) at the \(\rm CFS\) decreased slightly (4%) from the mean of the previous three years for the same period and increased by 8% at the \(\rm MS\)(Figure6b). Mean monthly day-time sensible heat flux (\(Q_{H}\), \(\rm MJ\;m^{-2}\)) was similar in each year for both sites (Figure6c) except from November 2015 to February 2016, where there were minima at the \(\rm MS\) associated with the cold weather and snowfall, which did not occur at the \(\rm CFS\), and May 2015 and 2016 due to high net radiation (Figure6a). At both sites, the monthly \(Q_{H}\) during late spring and summer (MJJ) of 2018 was only slightly higher (\(\sim\)20 \(\rm MJ\;m^{-2}\)) than the peak of the previous three years. Mean monthly day-time latent heat (\(Q_{LE}\), \(\rm MJ\;m^{-2}\)), was low in most winter months, except for the wet period in November 2015 to December 2015 at the \(\rm MS\), while it peaked in summer (Figure6d). In 2018 there was an exceptionally sharp increase in \(Q_{LE}\) from March to a peak in May, with rates in JJA higher than in any previous periods in the 4 years.

At the \(\rm CFS\), \(Q_{LE}\) was usually lower than at the \(\rm MS\), except for a period of 5 months after re-planting most likely due to increased soil evaporation from soil disturbance and accumulated surface water from forestry management operations. The Bowen ratio (\(\beta\)) varied seasonally between -2 to 3 with the minima during winter, usually between November to January, and a maximum in May (Figure6e). The mean late spring-summer \(\beta\)(May-August) for the first three years was between 1.41 and 1.59 at the \(\rm MS\) and 1.18 and 1.64 at the \(\rm CFS\). The dry spring and warm summer of 2018 reduced mean late spring-summer \(\beta\) by 45% at the \(\rm MS\) and 17% at the \(\rm CFS\) to 0.82 and 1.18 respectively. Day-time soil heat flux was a very small component of the energy balance, 3 and 6% of \(R_{n}\) at the \(\rm MS\) and \(\rm CFS\) respectively, but it was always higher at the \(\rm CFS\) during spring and summer (Figure6d). Carbon and water fluxes

Annual net ecosystem carbon exchange (\(NEE\)) in the mature stand was between -7.0 and -8.6 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) in the first three years (i.e., a strong \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink, Table[tab:carbon_summary]). In the drought year of 2018, it dropped by 30% from the previous three year mean. Annual gross primary production (\(GPP\)) was between 22.8 and 25.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) the first three years, then dropped by 5% in 2018 while \(R_{e}\) was between 15.7 and 16.6 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) and then increased by 7% in 2018. On average, approximately 40% of annual \(R_{e}\) was soil respiration (\(R_{s}\)).

The clear-fell site was a strong \(\rm CO_{2}\) source in the year after felling, releasing 7.1 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\), almost the same absolute amount as the net uptake at the mature site that year. Subsequently there was a rapid reduction in the \(\rm CO_{2}\) loss by 57% in the second and a further 68% in the third year (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). Gross primary production in the first year of the new clear-fell was very small (1.5 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\), \(<\)7% of the \(\rm MS\) value) but in the second year it was three times larger, (21% of the \(\rm MS\) value). In 2017, the year of ground preparation and replanting, \(GPP\) increased a further 65% but with little further change in 2018. Ecosystem respiration (\(R_{e}\)) at the \(\rm CFS\) was substantial in all four years, (although only 49-58% of that at the \(\rm MS\)) but in 2018 it was 28% higher than the mean of the previous three years. In 2015, 45% of the ecosystem respiration was from the soil at the \(\rm CFS\) and we assumed the same proportion for the subsequent years in order to estimate annual \(R_{s}\), assumption which may have introduced a bias in the calculated annual totals. The drought conditions in the late spring and summer of 2018 turned the \(\rm CFS\) back into a strong source equivalent to 41% of the net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake by the \(\rm MS\) in that year.

Figure[fig:carbon_water] shows the time course of monthly \(NEE\) at both sites and how it related to daily evapotranspiration, (\(ET\)). At the \(\rm MS\) the monthly \(NEE\) was at a minimum (maximum net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake) around July due to both high \(GPP\) and \(R_{e}\)(Figure[fig:carbon_water]a,b). \(GPP\) increased rapidly from February and March as solar radiation increased, through to peak values in June-August. During the drought of 2018, \(ET\) increased sharply between May and July (Figure[fig:carbon_water]d) to 105 \(\rm mm\;d^{-1}\), leading to a large drop in \(S_{w}\)(minimum of 23%, Figure1d) and reduction in \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake (i.e. \(NEE\) became less negative, Figure[fig:carbon_water]c) and in \(GPP\)(Figure[fig:carbon_water]a). Although precipitation returned to more normal values from August (Figure1c), \(S_{w}\) did not start to increase until September and did not reach pre-drought levels by the end of the year (Figure1d).

The monthly \(NEE\) values show that the clear-fell site was a \(\rm CO_{2}\) source throughout 2015 and 2016. In 2017 although the \(\rm CFS\) was a net source over the whole year \(NEE\) became slightly negative after April (Figure[fig:carbon_water]c) when \(GPP\) increased (Figure[fig:carbon_water]a). In the period after the replanting operations \(ET\) at the clear-fell site was slightly higher than \(ET\) in the mature stand. The monthly mean \(S_{w}\) was always higher at the \(\rm CFS\) than the \(\rm MS\) and showed only slight declines each summer, except in 2018 when the decline was more marked. There was no immediate large change in \(R_{e}\) after replanting operations, but rather \(R_{e}\) steadily increased as vegetation grew. The peak in \(ET\) during the 2018 drought was a month later than at the \(\rm MS\) but \(S_{w}\) was at its lowest in July in both sites.

In 2015-2017, annual evapotranspiration was 25% of the annual precipitation, but in 2018 \(ET\) increased by 69% at the \(\rm MS\) and 36% at the \(\rm CFS\) compared to the 2015-2017 mean (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). The drought of 2018 caused 27% of the daily \(S_{w}\) values at the \(\rm MS\) to drop below the minimum value of previous three years whereas at the \(\rm CFS\)\(S_{w}\) was reduced by only 6%.

Water use efficiency

Mean inherent water use efficiency calculated for dry days only (\(iW_{E}\)) during April-July in the first three years was between 28.7 and 42.1 \(\rm gC\,\allowbreak hPa\;kgH_{2}O^{-1}\) for the mature stand. Water use efficiency at the \(\rm MS\) was at its lowest during winter months, and highest in July (Figure[fig:carbon_water]e). It was high in February and March 2016 and November 2017 because of moderate rates of photosynthesis (Figure[fig:carbon_water]a). Between March and May 2018 \(iW_{E}\) decreased by 73% from the previous three year mean. The \(iW_{E}\) remained very low during summer and autumn due to the low soil moisture, which did not start to recover till after September (Figure1d). Calculations of \(iW_{E}\) for the clear-fell site resulted in a large number of gaps, making it difficult to see seasonal patterns. However, \(iW_{E}\) was predominately lower than for the \(\rm MS\) as the \(GPP\) was always small, and appeared to be increasing over time as \(GPP\) increased. During 2018 \(iW_{E}\) values for both sites were similar.

Discussion

Energy balance

Energy balance closure (\(EBC\)) at the mature stand was within previously reported values for Sitka spruce stands (Clement 2004). The author also reported a variability between different days of the year and day/night, with \(EBC\) ranging between 0.6 to 1.1. Grünwald and Bernhofer (2007) also reported annual closure between 0.76 and 0.94 for Norway spruce. We found our highest \(EBC\) to occur during the driest of the four years in our study, while Grünwald and Bernhofer (2007) did not note a particular improvement during their drought year of 2003.

At the \(\rm CFS\) the energy balance closure was higher than the values of 0.81 and 0.74 reported by Paul-Limoges et al. (2015) and Williams et al. (2013), respectively. Kowalski et al. (2004) reported higher \(EBC\) values for harvested sites which is also evident here when comparing the clear-fell to the mature stand. Energy balance closure in the first year was close to these reported values but it is likely it was overestimated as it was not measured for a complete annual cycle. The imbalance in energy exchange we found at the \(\rm MS\) was similar to the 20% reported across a range of vegetation types and climates (Wilson et al. 2002). The lack of energy balance closure can be influenced by instrument errors, canopy architecture, site topography (Clement 2004) or damping of high frequency fluctuations causing underestimation of latent heat (Goulden et al. 1996). At the \(\rm MS\) it is likely attributable to either an underestimation of turbulent fluxes (Amiro et al. 2006; Masseroni, Corbari, and Mancini 2014; Twine et al. 2000; Wilson et al. 2002) as decoupling and possible advection have reduced the energy fluxes measured at the top of the canopy or missing heat storage below and within the canopy. Another possible explanation for the lack of energy balance closure can be the different horizontal scale at which each energy component is measured (Foken 2008).

Energy balance closure improved during 2018 at both sites. Goulden et al. (1996) showed that for a closed path IRGA, damping of the signal at high frequencies cause a 20% reduction in evapotranspiration due to absorption and desorption on the walls of inlet tubing. Although our modified enclosed open path IRGAs were sampling air through a 1.5 m tube and although short, metal, and insulated (but not heated), it may be possible that less condensation inside the tube during the dry 2018 conditions reduced the high frequency damping and improved the estimation of latent heat and energy balance closure.

The systematic error of the \(EBC\) represented by the intercept was found to differ between the two sites (Table[tab:ebc]). As Masseroni, Corbari, and Mancini (2014) demonstrated in a study aiming to improve the energy balance closure over a maize field, the systematic error was reduced (intercept closer to zero) when unaccounted terms were included (e.g., soil heat storage, canopy heat storage and energy flux for photosynthesis). The authors also found the error was sensitive to friction velocity and vegetation height. The differences we observed between the two sites is probably a combination of storage and friction velocity. At the mature site, it is likely the dense canopy and low wind speeds created stable conditions and increased the heat storage in the stand. In determining the daily energy balance closure of the canopy, stem, bole space and soil storage component were not accounted for, although the influence on the daily \(EBC\) should be small. Furthermore, energy used for photosynthesis was also not considered. Future work should aim to quantify these missing elements of the energy balance which can reduce the systematic error and improve energy balance closure (Masseroni, Corbari, and Mancini 2014). At the clear-fell site the systematic error was smaller and closer to zero. Low friction velocities and lack of any canopy heat storage is the most likely explanation for the lower error.

The lower day-time \(R_{n}\) measured at the clear-fell site compared to the mature stand (Figure6 and [fig:diurnal_energy]) was partly caused by the 5-8% higher albedo, (declining over time as ground vegetation established), but also moderated by the higher surface temperatures at the exposed \(\rm CFS\). The range of monthly surface albedo we observed at the \(\rm MS\) was very close to typical measured or modelled values for boreal and northern temperate spruce stands (0.08-0.12; Betts and Ball 1997; Clement 2004; Betts, Desjardins, and Worth 2007; Lutz and Howarth 2014). Hovi, Lukeš, and Rautiainen (2017) reported mean modelled albedo values of 0.08 for spruce stands in Finland with little variation from May to October as we observed. A 3% winter increase in albedo was noted at the BOREAS spruce sites (Betts and Ball 1997) associated with snow cover on the ground, but such conditions occurred infrequently and for only short durations at our site. The albedo of the clear-fell site was close to the modelled spring/summer value of 0.16 reported by Lutz and Howarth (2014) for a clear-cut area in New Hampshire, N.E. USA.

The day-time mean \(\beta\) for the mature stand in the first three years (Figure6e) was close to the mid-day values of 1.15-2 reported by Clement (2004) for a Scottish Sitka spruce stand and 1.34 for a Norway spruce in Germany (Wilson et al. 2003). Grünwald and Bernhofer (2007) found lower \(\beta\) of between 0.6-0.7 for a slightly wetter but similar temperature site with older Norway spruce trees on more freely drained podzolic brown earth soil. Although a large component of the latent heat flux is dependent on the site conditions, the transpiration component depends on tree photosynthetic rates and stomatal control of water loss which are age and species dependent, which may explain the lower value of \(\beta\) observed by Grünwald and Bernhofer (2007). Drought conditions in 2018 caused \(\beta\) to drop closer to 0.7.

At the clear-fell site \(\beta\) was also mainly between 1 and 2, between spring and autumn. These low \(\beta\) values emphasise the cool, wet conditions at both sites, and show that the clear-fell site did not completely dry out even in the 2018 drought conditions. Only on one occasion during the warm dry May in 2016 \(\beta\) was close to 3 (Figure6e). Korkiakoski et al. (2019) also reported \(\beta\) between 1.5 and 2.5 for the first year after felling of a peatland site and between 0.5 and 1.5 for the second year. While we were not able to measure the energy balance immediately after felling, day-time \(\beta\) values in July-September were the same as the mature site.

The harvesting of the trees removed the very effective solar radiation absorbing surface of the conifer canopy and exposed the soil and litter surface causing an increase in albedo, approximately doubling at first (Figure6), reducing the net all-wave radiation receipt (Figure6 and [fig:diurnal_energy]). As vegetation cover increased, the albedo declined as expected.

Carbon balance

Early analysis showed measured net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake was overestimated compared to published values for spruce species in similar condition (Baldocchi, Chu, and Reichstein 2018; Clement, Jarvis, and Moncrieff 2012; Kowalski et al. 2004; Magnani et al. 2007; Saunders et al. 2012). Although in many situations this can be due to night-time stable wind conditions (Clement, Jarvis, and Moncrieff 2012; Papale et al. 2006), topographic flux loss (Clement, Jarvis, and Moncrieff 2012) or advective losses (Staebler and Fitzjarrald 2004; Hong et al. 2008), in our case the overestimation was due to canopy decoupling (Figure4). Recent studies highlight similar \(NEE\) overestimation in high \(L^{*}\) stands caused by a strong canopy decoupling effect (Jocher et al. 2017, 2018; Thomas et al. 2008, 2013). Our results provide additional evidence (Figure4) and underpin the importance of corrections using below canopy ecosystem respiration estimates (Figure5). Our assumption that photosynthesis under the canopy is zero is reasonable as there is no ground vegetation (except for occasional patches of moss) due to very little light penetrating the dense canopy. The values of the standard deviation of the wind speed above the canopy, \(\sigma_{w_{top}}\) used as a threshold for correcting \(NEE\) were higher than those used by Jocher et al. (2017, 2018) or Thomas et al. (2013) (0.33 and 0.45 \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\)) but we believe not unrealistic. The small range of \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) suggests very still wind conditions below the canopy as 95% of the wind speeds under the canopy were below 0.6 \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\)(data not shown). The methodology proposed by Thomas et al. (2008) depends on identifying the threshold as the point where the linear relationship between \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) and \(\sigma_{w_{top}}\) breaks. The low range of \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) resulted in only a small proportion of the data where there was a linear relationship between the bottom and top (Figure5). Similar analysis with data from Forest Research’s broadleaf forest \(\rm CO_{2}\) flux site in Hampshire (Wilkinson et al. 2012) showed \(\sigma_{w_{bottom}}\) in that stand was reaching values \(>\) 0.3 \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\) and up to 1.0 \(\rm m\;s^{-1}\) with a well-defined linear relationship between below- and above-canopy \(\sigma_{w}\)(Matthew Wilkinson, pers. communication) indicative of a more open and better coupled sub-canopy. We used a statistical method to minimise the sum of the root mean square error of the two linear regressions to calculate the \(\sigma_{w_{top}}\) threshold, which with the comparison with a well-coupled canopy increased our confidence that the applied corrections were justified. Uncorrected \(R_{e}\) was closer to values reported by other studies which suggests possible canopy de-coupling effects may have been ignored. In our study, we found that ignoring this effect would have introduced an underestimation (more negative) of annual \(NEE\) by 3.54 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\), introducing considerable bias in estimated annual carbon budgets. It is unknown if this error would propagate over the years at a constant rate or if it will vary in response to environmental conditions. We believe it is necessary for future flux monitoring studies to adopt measuring protocols for quantifying possible de-coupling effects. Furthermore, the use of process-based models and data assimilation techniques can quantify the long-term bias on annual budgets if such effects are unaccounted.

After corrections for decoupling, the 4-year annual mean \(NEE\) of -7.35 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)(Table[tab:carbon_summary]) for our 44-48 year old spruce stand was 50% higher than the -4.9 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) by the 41 year old spruce stand in 2001 (Kowalski et al. 2004) and the -5.5 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) maximum of a Sitka spruce stand across a 30 year rotation (Magnani et al. 2007) both in Harwood Forest on similar soil types. Kowalski et al. (2004) reported an annual rainfall of 950 \(\rm mm\), which is lower than the total in our dry year, 2018 (1162 \(\rm mm\)), with similar \(NEE\) of -5.6 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)(Table[tab:carbon_summary]). The 4-year mean was very close to the 5-year mean \(NEE\) of -7.2 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) reported by Clement, Jarvis, and Moncrieff (2012) for younger spruce stands (22 years old) on a similar soil type but a cooler (6.6 \(\rm ^{o}C\) annual mean) site in Griffin forest, 250 \(\rm km\) N from this study. Similar but slightly larger values for \(NEE\) were also reported by Saunders et al. (2012) who found a 24 year old stand in Ireland varied between -6.75 to -10.33 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) over 8 years with a mean of -8.55 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\). Black et al. (2007) reported \(NEE\) of -8.9 and -8.3 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) also from a stand in Ireland, which is very close to the net ecosystem exchange measured in our two wettest years. Net ecosystem exchange estimates from Black et al. (2007) and Saunders et al. (2012) closely resemble those from the two wettest years in this study, with variations likely attributable to differences in temperature between sites.

Annual gross photosynthesis (\(GPP\)) totals of the \(\rm MS\)(Table[tab:carbon_summary]) were also very close to the values reported by Saunders et al. (2012 range of 19.0 to 25.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)), Saunders et al. (2014, 23.5 \rm tC\;ha^{–1}\;yr^{–1}) and Kowalski et al. (2004, 19.7 \rm tC\;ha^{–1}\;yr^{–1}). Magnani et al. (2007) reported a lower \(GPP\) of 15.4 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\), but our stand is 15 years older and it is closer to its rotation end (i.e., it is close to the peak of mean annual growth increment) hence we expect higher growth rates. Our values of \(R_{e}\) at 15.7 - 17.5 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)(once corrected for periods of canopy decoupling) were higher than those reported in the literature. Kowalski et al. (2004) reported \(R_{e}\) of 13.9 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) and Magnani et al. (2007) of 12.7 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) while for the Irish stand it was between 10.9 and 16.2 (Saunders et al. 2012, 2014). Similarly, Grünwald and Bernhofer (2007) found \(R_{e}\) to range between 11.3 and 14.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) in a 118 year old Norway spruce stand; although that site had a similar mean air temperature (7.8 \(\rm ^{o}C\)). Annual soil respiration rates measured in this study (5.6-7.6 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)) were similar to those reported by Zerva et al. (2005) and Zerva and Mencuccini (2005) of 5.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) and 5.8 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) for first rotation 40 year old Sitka spruce stands and 5.4 tC ha-1 yr-1 for a 30 year old second rotation stand in Harwood Forest. Ball, Smith, and Moncrieff (2007) reported soil respiration for a 30 year old stand of 6.1 and 3.7 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) for two different years, an even larger interannual variation than we observed.

There are two studies published assessing the carbon balance of clear-felled stands which are comparable to ours. Kowalski et al. (2004) reported annual gross photosynthesis of 0.98 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) and ecosystem respiration of 11.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) for a site in Harwood Forest in the third year after clear-felling which resulted in a net \(\rm CO_{2}\) source of 1.12 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\). In our clear-fell site the net source was very similar in 2017, the year of replanting, (0.97 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)) although only 2 years after felling, but the component fluxes, \(GPP\) and \(R_{e}\), were both much larger, (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). Paul-Limoges et al. (2015) also found high \(R_{e}\) of 10.0 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)(similar to our 8.56 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)) in the first year after felling of a Canadian Douglas fir stand on a humic podzol \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) where planting immediately followed felling. Measured soil respiration at the \(\rm CFS\) was between 4.14 and 5.90 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) which is close to those previously reported for another clear-felled area in Harwood Forest (Zerva et al. 2005; Zerva and Mencuccini 2005, 5.6 and 4.1 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)), but lower than those reported by Ball, Smith, and Moncrieff (2007, 7.1 and 6.5 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)).

There was a reduction in soil respiration in the first year after felling compared to the mature stand from 6.34 to 3.34 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)(Table[tab:carbon_summary]). The reduction presumably arises because of the change of respiration from both below-ground autotrophic and heterotrophic processes to largely heterotrophic as decomposition continues. Data from the \(\rm CFS\) collected from 8 static dark chambers showed similar reduction in soil respiration (Yamulki et al. 2020) while soil sampling found significantly reduced live root biomass 4 months after felling ended (Vanguelova 2020, personal communication).

Our results are in agreement with other studies (Kowalski et al. 2004; Paul-Limoges et al. 2015; Korkiakoski et al. 2019) in temperate and boreal conifer forests showing that clear-felling turns a forest stand from strong \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink to a strong source. During the first year after felling the source was as large as the sink in the mature stand in 2015 in absolute terms (+7.05 versus -7.09 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) respectively, Table[tab:carbon_summary]). In British Columbia, Paul-Limoges et al. (2015) found that clear-felling Douglas fir stands doubled the \(\rm CO_{2}\) source compared to the sink prior to felling, although the source strength varied between two clear-fellings. This difference is likely to be because Harwood Forest is cooler with wetter soil than the warmer, podzolic soil reported by Paul-Limoges et al. (2015). In a southern Finland forest on a peat soil Korkiakoski et al. (2019) showed a slightly higher source of carbon after clear-felling (8.42 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)) probably because of the higher organic carbon content of their peatland soil.

The strength of \(\rm CO_{2}\) source in our clear-fell site was substantially reduced by in the second year to 3.06 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\). Paul-Limoges et al. (2015) found a similar strong reduction of 3 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\), and noted the recovery was due to the increase in existing ground vegetation. Korkiakoski et al. (2019) found a recolonisation of ground vegetation in the second year which reduced the source by 32%, compared to our 52% reduction. This is not unreasonable because of two main factors. First, there will have been some underestimation of the first year source because of the temperature dependent gap-filling applied before measurements started in 2015 and second, organo-mineral soil is better drained than deep peats, which allows both ground vegetation growth and natural regeneration of spruce, hence the stronger uptake.

Despite the site being a strong \(\rm CO_{2}\) source in the first year after felling there was substantial photosynthetic \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake (\(GPP\)) because of the vigorous colonisation from grasses, sedges, herbs and mosses, as previously reported by Zerva and Mencuccini (2005). A survey conducted on the site in August 2015 (data not shown) found a large amount of Juncus effusus, and the mosses Plagiothecium undulatum and Sphagnum sp. in wetter parts, colonising the site only six months after felling with regenerating spruce seedlings appearing in the second year. Kirby (1988) found that after clear-felling of conifer plantations in previous lowland mixed deciduous woodlands the abundance and richness of grass species increased immediately.

Carbon balance of clear-fell phase

Standard forestry practice in Britain recommends planting should occur within two to three years after felling (Murgatroyd and Saunders 2005) and the site was replanted in 2017, allowing further colonisation by ground vegetation and natural regeneration to increase photosynthesis during the second year (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). Gross primary production of the \(\rm CFS\) in the year of planting was a third of that of the \(\rm MS\). Kowalski et al. (2004) found their clear-fell Sitka spruce site had half the photosynthesis of a mature stand by the third year. However, both sites in the present study were more productive (Table[tab:carbon_summary]), and by the end of the third year the site was only a weak source (0.97 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)). It is also noteworthy that soil disturbance due to ground preparation and replanting was not enough to depress \(GPP\) suggesting a strong contribution by the ground vegetation. In 2018 the spring-summer warm and dry conditions reduced water availability and increased respiration increasing the source again (see discussion on drought below). Comparing the overall impact of the clear-fell management on the carbon balance over the four years of measurements, the total net uptake by the mature stand was 29.38 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) while total losses from the felled site were 13.38 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) with a loss:gain ratio of 46% indicating that the rate of \(\rm CO_{2}\) loss was almost half the carbon sequestered by the mature trees.

The time taken after afforestation for a site on organic or organo-mineral soil to act as a net \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink in the UK has been estimated for low-to-medium productivity (yield class 6-10) Sitka spruce stands as up to 5 years (Hargreaves, Milne, and Cannell 2003) and on peaty-gley soil between 10 and 12 years (Jarvis and Linder 2007; Jarvis et al. 2009; Minunno et al. 2010). These estimates were mainly based on limited \(\rm CO_{2}\) flux data and model assumptions. In this study, we aimed to quantify the time for sink recovery from longer-term flux measurements. Both Hargreaves, Milne, and Cannell (2003) and Jarvis et al. (2009) assumed a strong linear relationship of \(NEE\) with years since felling, whereas modelling using the simplified light use efficiency model 3-PGN by Minunno et al. (2010) showed a more gradual recovery. For our study, using a linear relationship between \(NEE\) and years since felling and ignoring the unusual drought year of 2018, the \(\rm CFS\) would have turned into a sink of 2.4 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) four years after felling. However, our data suggest this recovery pattern is not linear, and if assuming a logarithmic relationship the site would turn into a smaller \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink of 0.6 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\) in four years. Whichever assumption is used we conclude that highly productive Sitka spruce stands (yield class 12-18) on similar peaty-gley soils will most likely be a net \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink within five years post-felling, assuming losses due to dissolved or particulate organic carbon are negligible. In comparison, studies on Douglas fir forests found a sink recovery time of approximately 10 years (Humphreys et al. 2006; Paul-Limoges et al. 2015). However, the carbon balance time course will depend on the ground vegetation present, the extent of regeneration, the delay before replanting, nutrient loss, degree of disturbance during replanting and will be susceptible to extreme weather events such as droughts, which as our study showed can extend the carbon sink recovery time. Moreover, common site preparation practices such as draining and mounding will have a significant impact on the balance of the other greenhouse gases, \(\rm CH_{4}\) and \(\rm N_{2}O\) (Mojeremane, Rees, and Mencuccini 2012). To further enrich our knowledge on how clear-felling affects upland plantation forestry, more studies are necessary on a range of different productivity stands managed under typical forest operations.

The impact of the 2018 drought period

The impact of a drought on tree growth, carbon and energy exchange has been previously assessed for the European drought of 2003 (Ciais et al. 2005; Grünwald and Bernhofer 2007; Wen et al. 2010). In 2018, forests across central and northern Europe again experienced an extreme heatwave and drought (Bastos et al. 2020), and our measurements provide a new insight for a typical British Sitka spruce stand and associated clear-fell.

Whilst the soil of the mature stand was at field capacity at the start of 2018, as the year progressed soil water content declined due to a combination of reduced cloudiness and high solar radiation receipts (Figure1) and reduced precipitation in May, June and July (Figure1). Soil water content declined by June to lower levels than in previous years (Figure1d) and did not return to pre-drought levels increasing the risk for carry-over impacts on photosynthesis and net carbon uptake in the subsequent years (Aubinet et al. 2018). During the heatwave, vapour pressure deficit increased, reducing photosynthesis (Figure[fig:carbon_water]a and Table[tab:carbon_summary]) and water use efficiency (Figure[fig:carbon_water]e). The unusually high temperatures increased ecosystem respiration and particularly above-ground respiration causing a 30% reduction in net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake over the whole year (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). Our observations agree with a recent modelling study which suggested that the warmer spring in 2018 accelerated growth and evapotranspiration contributing to an earlier soil moisture depletion and amplifying the summer drought (Bastos et al. 2020).

Other studies found similar levels of reduction in net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake in mature spruce forest due to the 2003 drought (31%; Grünwald and Bernhofer 2007). The latter study also found a large increase in \(\beta\) during the 2003 drought doubling from approx. 0.6-0.7, while we found that during the drought more energy loss was by latent heat. We also found the drying soil affected the net radiation balance at the clear-fell as the exposed, drier soil surface (including a lot of needle litter) inhibited heat conduction and increased soil temperature (Figure1b), leading to higher long-wave radiation loss, so that the day-time net radiation was lower than in the previous years, and the difference compared to the mature stand was more than could be accounted for by the difference in albedo.

The high temperatures, solar radiation and \(D\)(Figure1) increased \(ET\) substantially at both sites (Figure[fig:carbon_water]d). Despite the reduction in \(ET\) after soil water reached its lowest level, \(GPP\) of the mature stand continued at high rates (Figure[fig:carbon_water]a) only a little lower than in the same months in previous years. This suggests sufficient stomatal opening for photosynthesis despite the dry soil and high water loss rates (leading to low water use efficiency; Figure[fig:carbon_water]e).

Drought inhibited the potential recovery of net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake and prolonged the period that the clear-fell was a source (Table[tab:carbon_summary]). Although summer \(GPP\) was slightly higher than the previous year, the annual \(GPP\) total was similar. However, the warm and dry conditions reduced soil water (Figure1d) and increased ecosystem respiration substantially, reducing net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake. If drought causes substantial seedling losses after planting this will further compound the impact. Further investigation is necessary to assess drought tolerance of seedlings and the impact of droughts on the recovery of the \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink.

Conclusions

This study provides information on the impact of the clear-fell/replanting silvicultural system on carbon, energy and water fluxes and the impact of drought on them by answering these four key questions.

  1. How did clear-felling influence the \(\rm CO_{2}\) source/sink dynamics in comparison to the closed-canopy stand?

    Clear-felling an upland Sitka spruce stand on peaty-gley soil resulted in a strong annual \(\rm CO_{2}\) emission (source) of similar magnitude to the net \(\rm CO_{2}\) uptake (or sink) of a mature stand (-7.09 compared to 7.05 \(\rm tC\;yr^{-1}\)). Growth of regenerating seedlings and ground vegetation reduced the source in the 2nd and 3rd years.

  2. How was the site energy and water balance affected by felling?

    The clear-felled area received less net radiation due to the higher albedo compared to the mature stand and had a warmer soil surface. The soil was wetter because of lower evapotranspiration from the ground surface, ground vegetation and seedlings. As vegetation recovered and new seedlings were established, more solar radiation was absorbed, and evapotranspiration increased.

  3. How many years for the clear-fell area to turn from \(\rm CO_{2}\) source to sink?

    In the year of replanting, two years after felling, the clear-fell site changed from a strong source to a weak source (0.97 \(\rm tC\;ha^{-1}\;yr^{-1}\)). Based on observed rates of net carbon uptake, similar sites on such organo-mineral soils will act as a sink within two years of planting if there is no impact of a drought.

  4. How did drought affect the sink/source dynamics for carbon, water, and energy at the two sites?

    The exceptionally warm and dry conditions of spring and summer 2018 reduced annual net carbon uptake of the mature stand by 30% compared to previous 3 years, through a small (5%) reduction in annual \(GPP\), and a small (8%) increase in \(R_{e}\). There was a large increase (28%) in \(R_{e}\) at the clear-fell site, which reduced the clear-fell site’s return towards being an annual net \(\rm CO_{2}\) sink. Evapotranspiration increased by 69% and 36%, from the mature stand and clear-fell sites, respectively.

Our results highlight the impact of clear-felling in Sitka spruce plantations on organo-mineral soils and have concentrated on the first few years post-harvest. Additional investigation is required to elucidate what is the short-term (e.g., weeks) impact of clear-felling on fluxes and if there is a large immediate pulse of \(\rm CO_{2}\), or other GHGs. More frequent and higher intensity drought will likely cause more carbon release from such clear-fell sites, limit vegetation growth and increase tree seedling mortality. Research is needed on lower-impact silvicultural management practices that may mitigate these issues.
